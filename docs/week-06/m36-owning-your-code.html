Owning Your Code: Proportionate Review Depth

The week's tools are on the bench: /diff, four checkpoints, a failure-mode taxonomy, a machine reviewer, a comment schema. This closing lesson is the principle that decides how hard to use them. It has two halves: why understanding the changes is what ownership means, and a sizing rule — review depth proportionate to how little the run was supervised. The quiz tests whether you can defend both, and apply the rule to cases.

M36 · Judgment Bloom: Evaluate Assessed by: Quiz 6

Objective: Explain why “understanding the changes is what it means to own your code,” and what review depth is proportionate to how little a run was supervised.

1 · What the ownership claim actually claims

Qiao's line — “understanding the changes is what it means to truly own your code” — is a definition, not a slogan. Unpack it by asking what ownership consists of when an agent did the typing:

  • Not authorship. You didn't write the lines, and the course has never pretended you did — Week 1 split decisions from typing, and typing was always the agent's share.
  • Not approval. Clicking merge transfers responsibility to you whether or not you understood what you merged. Approval without understanding is liability, not ownership.
  • Understanding. The thing that survives: when this code breaks at 2 a.m., when a teammate asks why it does X, when next month's feature has to modify it — the person who can answer is the person who owns it. Understanding is the only part of ownership that can't be delegated to the agent, because it's the part that supervises the agent.

This is why the week's habits are framed as ownership practices rather than defect hunts. Most /diff runs find nothing wrong — and aren't wasted, because the reading itself is what converts “the agent's changes” into “my changes.” The defect catch rate is the bonus; the understanding is the product.

The blame test

A one-line check of whether you own a change: if it ships a bug, is “the AI wrote that part” a sentence you'd say? If yes, you merged code you don't own. Nobody downstream — your user, your teammate, A6's grader — accepts the agent as a responsible party. The name on the merge is the owner; understanding is how the name gets earned.

2 · The proportionality rule

Understanding every change doesn't mean reviewing every change identically — the week already taught you otherwise. M32's thesis was “stop trying to read every line” because the checkpoints supervised the run as it happened. That “because” is the rule:

Review depth after the run should be proportionate to how little supervision happened during it.

Supervision and post-hoc review are the same commodity — your informed attention — delivered at different times. Every gate a change passed while it was being made (a plan you approved, a tool call you read before allowing, a failing test you watched go green, a scope bound you declared) is understanding already banked. Post-hoc review's job is to cover the gap between the understanding you have and the understanding ownership requires. Small gap, light review; no supervision, the review is the supervision, arriving late.

Note what the rule does not say: it never reaches zero. The floor is M31's unconditional habit — every task gets its /diff before commit — because “how much supervision happened” is itself a claim the diff verifies. The rule scales the depth above that floor.

3 · Defending the rule (this is the Evaluate skill)

The quiz won't ask you to recite the rule; it will hand you an argument against it and ask what's wrong. The three you'll meet:

  • “Tests passed, so deep review is redundant.” Tests certify behavior the tests describe — and M32's pitfall list showed the tests themselves are part of the diff. Green answers “does it work on the covered cases,” not “do I understand it” — and the 2 a.m. page arrives on an uncovered case.
  • “The machine reviewer looked, so I don't have to.” M34's findings are verified claims about defects. Ownership needs understanding of the whole change, including the 95% no finding touched. The reviewer deepens your review; it can't substitute for it, because its output was never “you now understand this.”
  • “Proportionality is just laziness with a rule attached.” Backwards: the rule raises the requirement exactly where corners get cut in practice — the long unattended runs that produce the most code with the least oversight. What it trims is double-paying attention on runs you already supervised. Fixed-depth review, by contrast, is mis-calibrated in both directions at once.

4 · Worked judgments

Case 1. Twenty-minute manual-mode session, plan approved, you read each edit as it happened, suite green. Classmate says: “now do a full line-by-line review with the M33 checklist.” Judgment: over-proportioned. The understanding is already banked — you watched it being built. The diff-gate pass (both /diff views, four questions) closes the gap; a second full read re-buys what you own. The real risk in this case is skipping the floor, not skimping on depth.

Case 2. Auto-mode ran ninety minutes while you were in a meeting; diff is +840/−210 across 14 files; tests green; you skim the stat view and merge. Judgment: under-proportioned, badly. Banked understanding is one scope declaration. Green tests don't close an 840-line comprehension gap (and the test diff itself was part of the unsupervised output). Proportionate here is the table's third row — per-turn replay, full checklist, machine pass, reproduce the behavior — and if that costs more time than supervising would have, that's the rule teaching you something about when to use auto mode.

Case 3. Your partner's A6 PR: their run, their supervision, your review. Judgment: your banked understanding is zero by construction — reviewer depth doesn't inherit the author's supervision. That's why A6 hands you the full instrument set (checklist, /code-review, comment schema) for a PR the author may have supervised closely. Ownership isn't transitive; each owner earns their own understanding.

Self-check

Evaluate-level: judge the argument, then compare your rationale with the model answer.

1. A classmate: “Ownership means I approved the merge. Understanding is nice-to-have — responsibility is about who clicked the button.” What's the strongest correction?

C. This is the definitional move in Qiao's line: ownership isn't the ceremony (approval) or the labor (typing) — it's the state of understanding that makes the responsibility dischargeable. The classmate is half-right, which is what makes it seductive: the button does assign responsibility; it just doesn't equip you for it. D fails Week 1's decision/typing split — authorship was never the criterion. B changes the subject to legal fictions; the test is practical (who can fix it), not juridical.

2. Same session type, two runs: (i) manual mode, every tool call read, plan approved; (ii) same task re-run in auto mode, unwatched, while you reviewed (i). Identical final diffs. Does the proportionality rule really prescribe different review depths for byte-identical diffs?

B. The cleanest way to see that the rule is epistemic, not artifact-based: review depth is a function of what you know, and supervision is how knowing got prepaid in (i). A has the right instinct for defect-hunting but the wrong target — the review's product is understanding, and understanding isn't a property of the bytes. C smuggles in a defect-rate claim the setup explicitly cancels (identical diffs). The same logic explains Case 3: a PR closely supervised by its author still meets a zero-knowledge reviewer.

3. “Since /code-review runs a verification step and I don't, the rational policy is: machine review at max effort on every PR, human review abolished.” Evaluate.

D. The argument's error is a category mistake — comparing the machine's defect-finding against the human's defect-finding and concluding the human is dominated, when the human review was never (only) hunting defects: it was earning ownership. On defects alone the machine is a strong complement (that was M34). B concedes the principle and haggles the price. C makes size the variable when the variable is what the human understands — which is why the rule keys on supervision, not line count alone.

4. End of A6, your partner reflects: “Honestly, my deepest understanding of my own PR came from responding to your review — re-reading my diff to answer your comments taught me things merging wouldn't have.” What does this observation get right about the week's design?

A. The partner has independently discovered why the ownership principle is the week's first lesson conceptually and its last page pedagogically: the tools are attention-structuring devices, and structured attention is how understanding gets built. Answering a five-part comment forces exactly the re-reading that ownership required anyway. B and D both reduce review to defect yield — the view section 1 spent the lesson dismantling (most /diff runs find nothing and are still not wasted). C would make understanding-gaps a scandal; the proportionality rule treats them as the normal quantity review exists to close.
