Agentic Coding with Claude Week 3 · Objective M14

Run a Grill Session

Theory's over. This lesson is the Assignment A3 procedure: set up the skill (or emulate it with one pasteable prompt), hold up your half of a forty-question interview, capture decisions as they resolve, and know when you've earned the right to stop.

M14 · Specification Bloom: Apply Assessed by: Assignment A3

Objective: Conduct a grill session in which Claude interviews the student until the design concept is shared, capturing decisions as they resolve.

1 · Set up: install or emulate

First, pick your course project — A3 asks you to choose it now, and it's the thing you'll be grilled about. One or two sentences is enough to start: Pocock notes you can begin with “a fully fleshed out idea, or just a vague couple of sentences.” Both are valid entry states; the session's job is to close the gap either way.

Then get the skill, either way A3 allows:

  • Install it. The skill lives in mattpocock/skills; the repo's README has current setup instructions (the skills are plain-markdown folders that go under ~/.claude/skills/). Once installed, type /grill-me followed by your project idea. Skills as a mechanism are Week 7's topic — for now it's enough that a skill is a reusable prompt the agent loads on demand.
  • Emulate it. The entire skill is six sentences — the discipline, not the packaging, is the value. Start a session and paste this, then your project idea:
The emulation prompt (verbatim from mattpocock/skills)
Interview me relentlessly about every aspect of this plan until we
reach a shared understanding. Walk down each branch of the design
tree, resolving dependencies between decisions one-by-one. For each
question, provide your recommended answer.

Ask the questions one at a time, waiting for feedback on each
question before continuing. Asking multiple questions at once is
bewildering.

If a *fact* can be found by exploring the codebase, look it up
rather than asking me. The *decisions*, though, are mine — put each
one to me and wait for my answer.

Do not enact the plan until I confirm we have reached a shared
understanding.

My project idea: [your one-to-two sentences here]
Start fresh

Run the grill in a fresh session (/clear first). Week 2's reason applies with extra force: the conversation this session produces is the design-concept asset, and you want it to contain your project's decisions — not the debris of whatever you were doing before. Since your course project starts from an empty repo, the “facts from the codebase” rule will rarely trigger; every question should be a real decision.

2 · Your half of the interview

The agent's behavior is scripted by the skill. Session quality now depends almost entirely on how you answer. Habits that separate a useful grill from 45 wasted minutes:

  • Answer one question at a time, in your own words. If Claude batches several questions, don't heroically answer them all — tell it to go back to one at a time. The skill calls batching “bewildering” for a reason: skimmed questions get shallow answers.
  • Use recommended answers as accelerators, not autopilot. Accepting a right recommendation costs one word. But when a recommendation is wrong for you, overriding it is the most valuable move in the session — that override is precisely the preference the agent could never have inferred. If you notice you've typed “yes” ten times in a row without thinking, slow down: you're being aligned to, not aligned with.
  • Push back on premises. The documented use cases include “when pushing back on assumptions is desired.” If a question presumes something you never decided (“When users log in…” — wait, is there login?), challenge the premise rather than answering inside it.
  • Steer coverage. Relentless doesn't mean complete. If you notice a branch the interview hasn't walked — error handling, data persistence, what v1 deliberately omits — say so: “we haven't covered X; grill me on it.” You own the map, not just the answers.
  • Don't let it build. If Claude drifts toward implementing (“shall I scaffold this?”), hold the line: the skill forbids enacting anything until you confirm shared understanding.

Expect the session to run tens of questions — Pocock's real sessions run 40–100 over roughly 45 minutes. Budget the time. The boring middle stretch is where the value hides: “walk down each branch of the design tree, resolving dependencies between decisions one-by-one” means the seventh question about deck storage exists because your answer to the third question about offline use constrained it.

3 · Capturing decisions as they resolve

The objective says “capturing decisions as they resolve” — present tense, during the session, not archaeology afterwards. The conversation itself is the primary artifact (“a rich conversation full of context, your actual ideas, and answers to difficult questions”), but Week 2 taught you exactly why a conversation is a fragile place to keep anything: context fills, sessions end, /clear happens. Externalize as you go:

  • Checkpoint summaries. Every 10–15 questions, ask: “List the decisions we've resolved so far, one line each.” Read the list — a wrong entry means a misunderstanding you can fix now, and catching one is cheaper here than in the PRD critique at Meeting 3.
  • A running decisions file. Have Claude append each resolved decision to a DECISIONS.md (or keep your own notes). This is the memento principle applied to specification: durable state outlives the window.

These captured decisions are the raw material for the next lesson — the PRD is written by synthesizing this session, explicitly not by interviewing you a second time.

4 · The stop test

A3's completion criterion is behavioral: the session ends when Claude can restate your design concept accurately. Run it as an actual test:

The stop test
Restate the design concept in your own words: what are we building,
for whom, what are the key decisions we've made, and what are we
deliberately not building?

Then grade the restatement like a skeptic. Anything wrong, missing, or fuzzier than your own understanding → not done; name the gap and let the interview resume on that branch. Restatement matches your mental model → confirm shared understanding out loud, and the session is over. Agreement ends a grill session; fatigue doesn't.

Hands-on checklist

This is the first half of Assignment A3 — do it for real, in your course-project repo:

Self-check

1. Twelve questions in, Claude asks three questions in a single turn. The best response is:

B. One-at-a-time is a rule of the session because batching produces skimming and shallow answers — the exact failure the grill exists to prevent. Enforcing the skill's own rules is part of your half of the interview; abandoning the session (C) or jumping to the stop test (D) forfeits alignment over a fixable drift.

2. Claude recommends “store data in localStorage — simplest for v1,” but you know you want users to switch devices. You're tired and tempted to type “yes.” What's actually at stake?

C. The session resolves dependencies one-by-one, so a wrong answer here silently shapes every later storage, sync, and auth question — and the agent can't catch it, because the cross-device requirement exists only in your head until you say it. Overrides are the highest-information moves in the session precisely because they're where your knowledge exceeds the agent's inference. “Change it later” (A) pays the misunderstanding tax the grill exists to avoid.

3. After 60 questions you're exhausted, and Claude's restatement of the design concept describes a review-scheduling behavior you never chose. You should:

D. The stop condition is accuracy, not effort — a restatement error is the test doing its job, revealing a branch that resolved wrongly (or never resolved). A ships a known misalignment into your PRD; B corrects the text without correcting the agent's model, which is what “shared” means; C throws away 59 questions of good alignment over one bad branch.

4. Why does the objective say to capture decisions as they resolve, rather than exporting them once at the end?

A. Both halves matter: reading a checkpoint summary is a mini stop-test (a wrong line is a live misunderstanding, cheapest to fix now), and the memento principle says durable decisions belong in files, not in a window that fills and clears. Nothing auto-deletes (B) and end summaries are fine (C) — they're just riskier as the only capture, because a 100-question session's early decisions may already be fuzzy by then.
