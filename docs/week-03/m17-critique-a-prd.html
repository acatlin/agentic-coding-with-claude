Agentic Coding with Claude Week 3 · Objective M17

Critique a PRD

Meeting 3 puts a classmate's PRD in your hands. This lesson gives you three lenses — ambiguity, missing acceptance criteria, scope creep — and a worked critique to practice on, so your feedback lands as engineering rather than opinion.

M17 · Judgment Bloom: Evaluate Assessed by: Meeting 3 exercise

Objective: Evaluate a peer's PRD for ambiguity, missing acceptance criteria, and scope creep.

1 · The three lenses

Each lens is a named flaw, a reason it matters specifically for an agent audience, and a mechanical test you can run on any sentence.

LensWhat it isWhy it matters for agentsThe test
Ambiguity Words two readers decode differently: fast, simple, intuitive, robust, correctly, etc., and so on Whatever the document leaves ambiguous, the build agent resolves silently (M13's failure mode, embedded in prose) Could two reasonable implementations both claim compliance while behaving differently? Then it's ambiguous.
Missing acceptance criteria A story or goal with no way to know it's done — nothing checkable backs it An uncheckable requirement gives the agent no target (M18): “looks done” becomes the stop signal For each story ask: “how would a stranger verify this, yes or no?” No answer in the document → flag it.
Scope creep Stories or goals beyond the stated problem; v2 features hiding in v1; content that contradicts the non-goals — or a non-goals section that's missing entirely Every extra story multiplies build and review work, and contradictions leave the agent to pick a side silently Trace each story to a goal; check each story and constraint against the non-goals; ask “could v1 ship without this?”

2 · A worked critique

Below is a condensed excerpt from “Riley's” PRD for StudyStack (course-written for this exercise). Read it with the three lenses, note what you'd flag, then work the judgment questions.

Riley's PRD.md — excerpt (flaws included)
# StudyStack — PRD (v1)

## Goals
- Make studying effortless and fun.
- A student can create decks of question/answer cards and review
  them on a spaced schedule.

## Non-goals (v1)
- No accounts or login.
- No mobile app.

## User stories
1. As a student, I want to create decks and add cards, so that I
   can organize my study material.
2. As a student, I want the app to feel snappy, so that I stay
   focused while reviewing.
3. As a student, I want cards scheduled with spaced repetition,
   so that I remember more with less time.
4. As a student, I want to share decks with classmates, so that
   we can study together.

## Constraints
- Plain HTML/JS, no build step. Use best practices.

## Acceptance criteria
- All features work correctly.
- Clicking a card flips it to show the answer.

Judgment 1 — Story 2 (“feel snappy”). Which critique is the strongest?

B. The model critique names the lens (ambiguity), demonstrates the test (two compliant-but-different implementations), and proposes the smallest fix (an operation plus a number) — turning the flag into a checkable criterion rather than a deletion. Performance is a legitimate v1 requirement (not A or C); D fails the test outright, since “snappy” on deck-load vs. card-flip vs. everything are different apps.

Judgment 2 — Story 4 (share decks with classmates). What should a critic flag?

C. The story doesn't just exceed scope, it contradicts the fence: “no accounts” and “share with classmates” can't both hold without infrastructure the document never mentions — and an agent implementing this PRD would have to pick a side silently. That's the highest-severity finding on the page. A is real but secondary; B shows why vague goals are dangerous (they can “justify” anything); D would paper over the contradiction with an equally uncheckable criterion.

Judgment 3 — The acceptance criteria section. Rank its two entries.

D. The card-flip line passes the stranger test: perform the click, observe the flip. “All features work correctly” delegates every definition of “correctly” back to the reader — for a build agent, that's no target at all. And the gap matters as much as the flaws: story 3 (the app's core mechanic!) is unverifiable as written, which is the “missing acceptance criteria” lens finding its most expensive instance. B is half-right about the gap but wrong to dismiss the flip criterion.

3 · Delivering the critique

Meeting 3's exercise is live, face to face. A protocol that keeps it useful and survivable:

  1. Open with what's specified well. A tight non-goals section or a genuinely checkable criterion deserves naming — it shows the author (and you) what the standard looks like.
  2. Phrase findings as verification questions, not verdicts. “How would we know the scheduler works?” does more than “your criteria are weak” — it hands the author the stranger test instead of a grade.
  3. Every flag cites a sentence. Critique the document, not the idea. “Story 4 vs. non-goal 1” is checkable; “the scope feels big” is vibes — hold your critique to the same standard the M12 write-ups were held to.
  4. Propose the smallest fix. Replace the adjective with a number; add the one missing criterion; move the story to non-goals. Smallest-fix framing keeps the author in charge of their own design.
  5. Critique to the destination standard, not the blueprint standard. M16 applies to critics too: flag what would make the build agent or the author diverge — not everything that could conceivably be added. “You could also specify…” is how peer review pressures people into the over-polishing M16 warned against. If the document is unambiguous about its decisions, checkable, and coherent, it's done — say so.
Receiving it

Your PRD gets the same treatment. The productive posture: every flag caught at the meeting is one the build agent won't resolve silently next week — your critics are debugging your spec while bugs still cost sentences. Fold accepted fixes into PRD.md and commit, so the revision is visible history (and expect the document to still rot later; that's fine).

Self-check

1. A constraint reads: “Use best practices.” Which lens, and what's the fix?

C. “Best practices” is the constraint-section cousin of “works correctly” — every implementer, human or agent, claims compliance while doing different things. The fix isn't a checkbox (B keeps the vagueness, just relocated), it's recovering the concrete decisions the phrase is hiding. If the grill never settled them, the critique just found a branch that needs grilling — also a useful finding.

2. A classmate's PRD is two tight pages: decisions unambiguous, every story numbered with a checkable criterion, stories trace to goals, non-goals coherent. Your strongest contribution at the meeting is:

A. Evaluation means rendering the supported verdict, and sometimes the verdict is “done.” B manufactures findings — the reviewer failure mode where flags get invented because flags were requested; C is the over-polish pressure M16 warned about, now applied socially. A specific “this criterion is the model — checkable by a stranger” also teaches the room what good looks like, which is the exercise's actual point.

3. Why does the course schedule the peer critique before the build instead of after it?

B. It's the week's economics applied one last time: alignment defects are cheapest at the document stage, and the critique is a second grill — fresh eyes walking branches the author's session missed. After the build, an ambiguity isn't a flag anymore; it's whatever behavior the agent picked, waiting in the diff for review (Week 6's much more expensive version of the same catch).

4. Which finding is scope creep rather than ambiguity or a missing criterion?

D. The story fails the traceability test — it serves no stated goal, so it's growth beyond the agreed problem: extra build work, extra review surface, and precedent for more. A and C are ambiguity (unmeasurable words); B is the missing-criteria lens. Note the fix for D is cheap and non-destructive: move it to non-goals with a “v2 candidate” note, which records the idea without widening the destination.
