Interrupt, or Let It Finish?

M30 gave you the controls; this lesson is about the trigger finger. Mid-run, something looks off. Do you hit Esc now, or let the run complete and judge the finished diff? Both answers are sometimes right — this is a judgment call with real criteria, and Meeting 5 will ask you to defend yours.

M29 · Judgment Bloom: Evaluate Assessed by: Meeting 5 discussion

Objective: Recognize when to interrupt and redirect an agent mid-task vs. let it finish and review the diff.

1 · The two error costs

Every mid-run “that looks wrong” moment carries two possible mistakes, and they cost differently:

  • Interrupting too late. The docs' baseline: “Correct Claude as soon as you notice it going off track” — because a wrong direction compounds. Every minute after a misread spec or a wrong-file start is work that review will only sentence to rework. M30's table row: caught during, it costs a redirect; caught after, it costs everything built on it.
  • Interrupting too often. The docs' counterweight is just as documented: “Correcting over and over… Context is polluted with failed approaches,” and after two failed corrections on the same issue the play is /clear and a better prompt, not a third correction. Add the M30 blind spot — a half-built implementation legitimately looks wrong; scaffolding isn't drift — and reflexive Esc-tapping becomes its own failure mode: you pay interruption cost for corrections that the run's next step would have made unnecessary.

So the judgment reduces to one question: is what I'm seeing a wrong direction, or an unfinished middle? Directions compound — interrupt. Middles resolve — wait, and let the diff be judged whole.

Two structural notes behind the table. First, supervision should scale with stakes, not suspicion: a task with a strong verification gate (tests, plan contract, small blast radius) earns a longer leash — the same trade that let you loosen permission modes in M26. Second, letting it finish buys unbiased review: the finished diff can be judged whole, even by a fresh context — the best-practices guide notes a fresh reviewer “won't be biased toward code it just wrote,” which is where Week 6 picks up.

3 · The cheapest interrupt is at the plan

The judgment gets easier the earlier the checkpoint, and the plan gate is the earliest one. Correcting a plan step costs a sentence; correcting its implementation costs a rewind. This is why the approval dialog's “No, keep planning” exists — and why the richest version of it, Ultraplan's browser review, is built entirely around approve-with-comments: “highlight any passage and leave a comment for Claude to address,” react to sections, then “iterate as many times as needed before choosing where to execute.” Comment-and-revise at the plan is interruption with all of the correction and none of the wasted work — spend your steering there, and the mid-implementation question comes up less at all.

4 · Worked judgment: three moments from one session

You're implementing “user can flip a card to reveal its answer,” plan approved, in acceptEdits.

Moment 1: Claude opens scheduler.ts — a file the plan never mentions — and starts editing. Call: interrupt. This is drift from a decision already made; the plan is the scope contract, and “why are we in scheduler.ts?” costs one line. If there's a real reason, it should become a plan revision, not a surprise.

Moment 2: The flip renders, but the card's styling is broken and an event handler is dangling. Call: let it finish. The plan's remaining steps cover wiring and tests; this is a middle. Judge the diff when the check is green — interrupting here reviews scaffolding.

Moment 3: For the second time, you've explained that state lives in the deck store, and Claude has again introduced component-local state. Call: stop correcting. One more correction would be the third; the documented move is /clear and a fresh session whose prompt (or CLAUDE.md line) states the state convention up front — the failure was in the spec layer, and that's where the fix goes.

Self-check

Evaluate-level: each question asks you to make the call, then compare your rationale with the model answer.

1. Five minutes into a run, Claude states it will store flip-state in localStorage. Your PRD — discussed at length in the grill session — put all card state in the deck store. Claude is now scaffolding the localStorage version. Make the call.

C. Direction, not middle: the wrong choice is load-bearing and already decided against, so waiting converts minutes of divergence into a fully-built wrong artifact. A confuses openness with drift — the time for “maybe localStorage” was the grill session. B is sunk-cost logic inverted: the five minutes are gone either way; the question is whether the next thirty compound the error. D over-fires: one clean redirect with preserved context is exactly what Esc is for; /clear is for corrections that keep failing.

2. Mid-run under M27's loop, you glance over: the implementation handles none of the edge cases, and a test is currently failing. Instinct says grab the wheel. Make the call.

B. The strongest let-it-finish case: a verification gate you trust is actively doing the supervision. Red is what the red–green cycle looks like from the outside, and “minimal code, nothing speculative” guarantees early cycles look incomplete. A misreads the loop's normal state as failure. C batches the tests — M27's documented anti-pattern. D unwinds correct process because it looked untidy mid-flight.

3. Claude presents a plan for your second A5 issue. Steps 1–4 are right; step 5 bundles an unrelated refactor. You're deciding between approving (you'll catch it in diff review) and pushing back now. Make the call.

D. Same judgment as question 1, moved one gate earlier — which only strengthens it: at the plan, correction costs a comment and zero work is discarded. That's the entire approve-with-comments idea. C is strictly worse than D — it plans an interruption it could have made free, and relies on you catching the moment. A approves known scope drift into the contract that M25b then enforces. B discards four good steps; revision, not rejection, is what “keep planning” is for.

4. A classmate's policy: “I watch every tool call and Esc the moment anything looks unfamiliar. It's my code; constant intervention is just diligence.” Evaluate the policy.

A. The policy treats “unfamiliar” as “wrong,” but unfinished work is unfamiliar by nature — that's M30's blind-spot row. The deeper miss is instrumental: the week built gates (plan approval, red–green, evidence-before-commit) precisely so a human doesn't have to adjudicate every step live. B restates the policy; it's the tenth-approval effect from M26 in interrupt form — constant vigilance degrades into noise. C names a cost Esc doesn't have (context survives). D is the opposite over-rotation; question 1 is a mandatory interrupt.
